## 2. One-Paragraph Project Summary

The project builds a complete reinforcement-learning training pipeline for a planar two-degree-of-freedom robotic arm. It uses Gymnasium for the environment standard and Stable-Baselines3 for the algorithms (PPO is the primary algorithm at present, with SAC and A2C also wired up). The system is composed of (i) a custom Gymnasium environment, `ArmTaskEnv`, in which the arm must learn to move from a vertical-down resting pose to a horizontal goal pose; (ii) a Tkinter + matplotlib GUI for interactive arm control and visualisation; (iii) a second Tkinter GUI for monitoring training metrics in real time; and (iv) a physics-grounded reward function whose four penalty terms are derived from established literature in classical mechanics, motor control, and computational neuroscience. There is also a parallel 3-D scaffold (`three_d/`) that mirrors the 2-D structure but is incomplete.

## 5. The Four Physics-Grounded Reward Penalty Terms

This is the core scientific contribution and the most important thing to understand before modifying the reward. All four constraints are implemented in `src/rl_armMotion/two_d/environments/task_env.py` with full academic citations in the source-code comments.

| Symbol | Quantity | Penalty form | Citation |
|--------|----------|--------------|----------|
| **A** | Gravitational potential energy | `-w_A · (m₁·g·h_com1 + m₂·g·h_com2)` | Spong, Hutchinson, Vidyasagar (2020) — *Robot Modeling and Control* |
| **C** | Sum-of-squared joint accelerations | `-w_C · Σ(α_i²)` | Uno, Kawato, Suzuki (1989) — minimum-torque-change |
| **J** | Mechanical energy expenditure | `-w_J · Σ\|τ_i · ω_i\|·dt` | Alexander (1984) — minimum-energy biological motor control |
| **K** | Jerk (third derivative of position) | `-w_K · Σ(j_i²)` | Flash & Hogan (1985) — minimum-jerk model |

The principal commits implementing these are `bf6c899` (the structural correction) and `d89cd2b` (full citation comments). Do not change the structural form of these penalties without first reading the cited papers in `docs/references/`.

## 7. The Single Most Important Next Step — Fischer 2021 Integration

The user's intent, stated explicitly in earlier sessions, is to implement the methodology of Fischer et al. (2021). The paper PDF is at `docs/references/Fischer_2021_ScientificReports_RL_biomechanical_arm.pdf`. A summary of the paper appears as Section T of the reference report and as Section 10 of the progress report.

### 7.1 What Fischer et al. (2021) Did

Fischer et al. trained a 7-DOF biomechanical upper-extremity model in MuJoCo using **Soft Actor-Critic (SAC)**. They used **adaptive curriculum learning**: the goal-tolerance radius starts at 60 cm and is shrunk progressively to 2 cm as the agent's success rate improves. They initialised the policy with a **motor-babbling** phase. They validated emergent behaviour against two biological benchmarks: **Fitts' Law** (R² = 0.9986) and the **2/3 Power Law** (R = 0.84).

### 7.2 What Has Been Adopted So Far

The reward function (Section 5 above) is in part informed by the same biomechanical literature Fischer cites. That foundation is in place. **Nothing else from Fischer is yet implemented.**

### 7.3 Concrete Next-Step Plan

A natural sequence — each step is a separate, reviewable commit on a new branch (e.g. `claude/fischer-integration`):

1. **Switch primary algorithm to SAC.** A `SAC` wrapper is already present at `src/rl_armMotion/two_d/models/agents/sac_agent.py`. Wire it into `training_gui.py` as the default, and add a SAC-specific trainer config.
2. **Implement adaptive goal-tolerance curriculum.** Add a `goal_tolerance` attribute to `ArmTaskEnv` that starts at 0.6 m (60 cm in workspace units, scaled to the 2-DOF arm's reach) and decays whenever the rolling success rate over the last *N* episodes exceeds a threshold (Fischer used 80 %). Log the current tolerance in the info dict.
3. **Add a motor-babbling pre-training phase.** Before policy learning, run *N* episodes of uniformly random actions and store the (s, a, r, s′) tuples in the SAC replay buffer.
4. **Implement Fitts' Law validation.** Run a grid of goal-distance × goal-tolerance combinations, log mean movement time per combination, fit MT = a + b·log₂(2D/W), and verify R² approaches Fischer's 0.9986.
5. **Implement 2/3 Power Law validation.** Log end-effector velocity and curvature on each step; fit `log V = α + β·log C` over the trajectory and verify β ≈ –1/3 (equivalently R between V^(1/3) and curvature ≈ 0.84).
6. **(Optional, large)** Migrate to a 3-DOF or 7-DOF arm. The 3-D scaffold in `three_d/` is the natural place.

Each of these can be implemented on top of the existing 2-DOF `ArmTaskEnv` without rewriting the foundation. Steps 1–5 should be feasible without leaving Python + Stable-Baselines3 + Gymnasium.

## 9. Important Historical Context (Things Not To Repeat)

### 9.1 The Forward Kinematics Bug

Early in the project the forward-kinematics formula treated the multi-link arm as a single rigid rod that rotated about the base. Joint-2 angle changes therefore appeared to alter the *visual length* of joint-1's link. This was diagnosed and fixed in `src/rl_armMotion/two_d/utils/arm_kinematics.py`. The correct formulation is a step-by-step accumulation: position[i] = position[i-1] + length[i] · [cos(global_angle[i]), sin(global_angle[i])]. Do not refactor this code without understanding why it is correct.

### 9.2 The Pickle Closure Bug

`linear_decay()` originally returned a local closure as a learning-rate schedule, and `pickle` could not serialise it. This was replaced with a top-level callable class `LinearDecaySchedule` in `src/rl_armMotion/two_d/models/trainers.py`. Any future schedules or callbacks placed in hyperparameters must be pickle-safe — top-level classes only, never nested closures.

### 9.3 The Eager-Import Dependency Coupling

Top-level imports of training modules in package `__init__.py` files used to drag in `stable_baselines3` even for visualisation-only workflows. This caused `app_3d.py` to fail with `ModuleNotFoundError` on machines without SB3 installed. The fix was to make the imports lazy via `__getattr__` in `three_d/gui/__init__.py` and to remove the eager imports from the root `__init__.py`. Do not re-introduce eager top-level imports of heavy dependencies.

### 9.4 The Tkinter `update_idletasks()` Crash

Forcing `update_idletasks()` from within Matplotlib idle-draw callbacks during config-load triggered `Fatal Python error: PyEval_RestoreThread … GIL is released`. The fix was to use deferred refresh (`root.after_idle(...)`) instead of forced redraws. Do not call `update_idletasks()` from within Matplotlib draw callbacks.

### 9.5 OpenMP SHM Errors in Sandboxed Execution

Some sandbox environments emit `OMP: Error #179: Function Can't open SHM2 failed` when importing NumPy/SciPy/Stable-Baselines3 stacks. This is environmental, not a code bug. If runtime checks fail with this error, fall back to `python -m py_compile` for static validation.
